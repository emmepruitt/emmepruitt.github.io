It is never too soon for NFL fantasy football fans to start looking into who they are going to draft to their team in the upcoming season.

Using LightGBM to get the fastest results, I used the metrics total yards per reception, total receptions, total touchdowns, the player’s rating and what pick they were in the 2023 NFL draft. I used total yards per reception, total receptions, and total touchdowns because these numbers are all important to how well a receiver can catch the ball, how far they can get down the field, and if they can put points on the board with their receptions. Additionally, I used the player’s rating and what pick they were to see what the NFL thought of the player, and if they were good enough, fast enough, strong enough to be picked in the first round.

This years wide receiver draft class was pretty impressive, and it will be interesting to see how each player performs in the league. Using machine learning and metrics that I chose, I am going to tell you what five wide receiver rookies you should draft to your team this season.

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(tidymodels)
library(cfbfastR)
library(janitor)
library(bonsai)
library(gt)

set.seed(1234)

wrstats <- read_csv("wrdraftedstatswithratingsfixed.csv") 

currentwrstats <- read_csv("https://mattwaite.github.io/sportsdatafiles/currentwrstats.csv")

wrselected <- wrstats %>%
  select(
    name,
    year,
    college_team,
    nfl_team,
    overall,
    total_yards,
    total_ypr,
    total_receptions,
    total_touchdowns,
    rating,
    pick,
    FantPt
  ) %>% na.omit()

player_split <- initial_split(wrselected, prop = .8)
player_train <- training(player_split)
player_test <- testing(player_split)

player_recipe <- 
  recipe(FantPt ~ ., data = player_train) %>%
  update_role(name, year, college_team, nfl_team, new_role = "ID")

lightgbm_mod <- 
  boost_tree() %>%
  set_engine("lightgbm") %>%
  set_mode(mode = "regression")

lightgbm_workflow <- 
  workflow() %>% 
  add_model(lightgbm_mod) %>% 
  add_recipe(player_recipe)

lightgbm_fit <- 
  lightgbm_workflow %>% 
  fit(data = player_train)

lightgbmpredict <- 
  lightgbm_fit %>% 
  predict(new_data = player_train) %>%
  bind_cols(player_train) 

lightgbmpredict <- 
  lightgbm_fit %>% 
  predict(new_data = player_test) %>%
  bind_cols(player_test) 

currentwrselected <- currentwrstats %>%
  select(
    name,
    year,
    college_team,
    nfl_team,
    overall,
    total_yards,
    total_ypr,
    total_receptions,
    total_touchdowns,
    rating,
    pick
  ) %>% na.omit()

currentlightgbmpredict <- 
  lightgbm_fit %>% 
  predict(new_data = currentwrselected) %>%
  bind_cols(currentwrselected) 

currentlightgbmpredict %>% 
  top_n(5, wt=.pred) %>%
  select(name, nfl_team, overall, .pred) %>%
  arrange(desc(.pred)) %>%
  gt()

1. Jordan Addison

My top pick from the 2023 WR rookie class is Jordan Addison. Addison, the 23rd overall pick selected by the Minnesota Viking’s, has a projected fantasy point total of 115.31.

The former Pitt/USC player totaled 219 receptions, 30 touchdowns and averaged 14.3 yards per reception. Addison was the winner of the Fred Biletnikoff Award (Outstanding Receiver) in 2021.

Allen Lazard, who produced 115 fantasy points in the 2022 season, is who Jordan Addison can be compared to. Lazard, who started in all 15 games he appeared in last year, averaged 13.3 yards per reception, and 6 touchdowns for the Green Bay Packers.

Jordan Addison will be a great fit with the Viking’s offense next year, and make an immediate impact.

2. Quentin Johnston

My second pick from the 2023 WR rookie class is Quentin Johnston. Johnston, the 21st overall pick selected by the Chargers, has a projected fantasy point total of 114.36.

Former TCU player totaled 115 receptions, 16 touchdowns, and averaged 19.0 yards per reception.

Quentin Johnston is a big wide receiver, and is comparable to Mike Williams, receiver for the LA Chargers, who started in all 13 games he appeared in for the Chargers. Dotson produced 114 fantasy points last year in those 13 games.

3. Jaxon Smith-Njigba

My third pick from the 2023 WR rookie class is Jaxon Smith-Njigba (Ohio State). Smith-Njigba, the 20th overall pick, selected by the Seahawks, has a projected fantasy point total of 109.46.

The Buckeye was #1 in total receptions and receiving yards in the Big Ten in 2021. Smith-Njigba had 95 receptions, 9 touchdowns and averaged 16.9 yards per reception in the 2021 season.

Tyler Boyd, receiver for the Cincinnati Bengals produced 109 fantasy points last year. With Smith-Njigba predicted to have 109 points in the 2023 season,and being the first receiver to be selected in the draft, I would definitely keep him on my radar.

4. Jalin Hyatt

My fourth pick from the 2023 WR rookie class is Jalin Hyatt. Hyatt, the 73rd overall pick selected by the Giants, has a projected fantasy point total of 97.74.

The Tennessee Volunteer totaled 108 receptions, 19 touchdowns, and averaged 16.4 yards per reception in his career.

Similar to Mack Hollins, LA Rams receiver, should produce 97 fantasy points for anyone’s team. Hyatt will be a reliable fantasy pick.

5. Jayden Reed

To conclude my top 5 wide receiver rookie class, we have Jayden Reed. Reed, the 50th overall pick, selected by the Green Bay Packers, has a projected fantasy point total of 96.79.

After spending one year with Western Michigan, Reed transferred to Michigan state and had a career 203 receptions, 27 touchdowns and averaged 14.1 yards per reception.

Jayden Reed can be compared to Vikings player Jahan Dotson, who in the 2022 NFL season produced 96 fantasy points. Dotson started in 10 of 12 games last year, and averaged 14.94 yards per reception and one touchdown.

Reed will be an important play maker for Green Bay this upcoming season.

These five rookie receivers are going to have a breakout year in the 2023 NFL season. These five players have had impressive collegiate careers, and have been predicted to have even more successful NFL career’s. These five receivers all have the stats to prove them valuable to anyone’s fantasy team and should not be overlooked.
